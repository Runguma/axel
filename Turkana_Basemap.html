    Turkana County

    At 77,000 sqkm, Turkana County is the second largest county, covering more than 13% of Kenya’s surface. The vast land in the northwest of Kenya is an awakening economic giant, for beneath its surface lie huge oil deposits that are currently explored and will see extraction in an industrial scale during the years to come – a windfall for a county with a population of slightly over 1 million. Besides, Turkana boasts plenty of wind, sun, flowing water and underground steam for geothermal power which could make it literally a powerhouse in sustainable energies, too.

    SOCIAL AND DEMOGRAPHIC CONTEXT.

    Turkana is the second largest of Kenya’s 47 counties.

    Kakuma and Kalobeyei refugee camps comprise 17% of Turkana County’s population and 45% of Turkana West sub county’s population. The refugees contribute to the population density of Turkana West Sub-county.

    Over the past 40 years, Turkana County has experienced high rates of population growth. However the KNBS 2019 census data suggests that this growth rate has slowed substantially.

    Turkana County has a particularly young population profile (population under 19 = 60% of population)

    Turkana county has a decreasing dependency ratio - infrastructure (employment, education and health) needed to capitalise on this.

    LOCATION AND CONNECTIVITY.

    Turkana county is in a strategic position and sharing its borders with three countries - chance to link trade among the three countries and boost bilateral trade agreements.

    Turkana is cut off from the rest of the country due to lack of infrastructure in terms of road and rail network. LAPSSET Corridor will increase connectivity to rest of Kenya.

    Location within the economic corridors that links Kenya, Ethiopia and South Sudan has not been capitalised upon.

    Proportion of refugee population (based on March 2020 figures) moving legally from Kakuma per year is approximately 2%.

    About us

    This programme aims to utilise Urban and Spatial Planning as a tool by which to bridge the gap between humanitarian and development activities. UN-Habitat headquarters, regional and country offices, and several partners deploy a wide range of planning projects and tools in different countries to bring together diverse solutions to address the commitments within the Global Compact on Refugees, Global Compact for Migration, Agenda 2030 and the Sustainable Development Goals, which include exploring “Alternatives to Camps”, supporting the paradigm shift towards the integration of refugee and host communities in combined programming, and development of sustainable and long-term solutions as part of multi-actor and comprehensive responses to “Leave No One Behind”, ensuring the most vulnerable populations are promoted and protected..
